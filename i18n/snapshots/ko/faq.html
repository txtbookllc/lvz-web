Screen magnification on Windows, and how Low Vision Zoom fits

Choosing a magnifier

What is the best screen magnifier for Windows?

There is no single best one — it depends on what you need, and the right first step is free. Every Windows PC already includes Windows Magnifier (press the Windows key + Plus to start it); for many people it is enough. If you need your screen read aloud, very high magnification, color filters, or braille, a full assistive-technology suite such as ZoomText or SuperNova is the right category. If what you want is smooth, calm zoom that follows your cursor while you work, without settings to configure, Low Vision Zoom is built for that specific case and costs $9.99 once. Because magnification is personal, try any option before you pay. See our honest comparison.

Is there an alternative to Windows Magnifier?

Yes. Besides Microsoft's built-in Windows Magnifier, third-party magnifiers for Windows include ZoomText and SuperNova (full low-vision suites with speech and reading tools), the free Magnifixer, and Low Vision Zoom, a small $9.99 utility focused on smooth, cursor-anchored zoom. People usually look for an alternative when they want a smoother feel, zoom centered on the mouse pointer, or speech and color features the built-in tool does not offer. Which alternative fits depends on which of those you need — the comparison page lays them out side by side.

Is there a cheaper alternative to ZoomText?

It depends on which ZoomText features you rely on. ZoomText is a full low-vision suite with speech, a document reader, extensive color and focus enhancements, and very high magnification, typically sold as an annual license. If you use those reading and enhancement features, the honest answer is that a cheaper tool will not replace them. If you mainly want smooth magnification that follows your cursor, Low Vision Zoom does that one thing for a one-time $9.99 — but it has no speech, no reading, no color filters, and stops at 5×. Windows Magnifier is also free and more capable than many people realize.

Does Low Vision Zoom read text aloud?

No. Low Vision Zoom is magnification only. It has no text-to-speech, no document reader, and no screen reader. If you need your screen read aloud, look at ZoomText Magnifier/Reader, SuperNova in a speech or screen-reader edition, or Windows' built-in Narrator, which is free.

How is Low Vision Zoom different from the built-in Windows Magnifier?

Windows Magnifier is free, built in, and capable, and many people should try it first. Low Vision Zoom differs in feel rather than feature count. Its zoom is continuous and eases smoothly to the new level instead of stepping, it pivots around the exact point under your mouse cursor so what you are looking at stays put, and it holds the view still while your cursor roams the middle of the screen. It has no settings and lives in the system tray. It is also more limited than Windows Magnifier in some ways: it has no speech, no color filters, and it stops at 5×. There is more on the reasoning behind the smooth motion in why smooth magnification is easier to follow.

Using Low Vision Zoom

How do I zoom in on Windows 11?

Windows has a built-in magnifier: hold the Windows key and press Plus to zoom in, Minus to zoom out, and Windows key + Esc to turn it off. You can also make everything permanently larger under Settings → System → Display → Scale, or enlarge just text under Settings → Accessibility → Text size. With Low Vision Zoom installed, you hold Ctrl and roll the mouse wheel to zoom smoothly anywhere in Windows, or use Ctrl + Alt + Plus / Minus if you have no mouse wheel.

Is there a magnifier that zooms with the mouse wheel?

Yes. Low Vision Zoom zooms with Ctrl + the mouse wheel: hold Ctrl and roll the wheel forward to zoom in, backward to zoom out. The zoom is proportional, so you go exactly as far and as fast as you turn the wheel, and it eases smoothly to the new level while staying centered on the point under your pointer. Ctrl + Shift + Scroll down snaps back to normal. This is the main way the app is designed to be used.

Does Low Vision Zoom work with multiple monitors?

Yes. It magnifies the whole Windows desktop as one continuous space and follows your cursor from one monitor to the next, rather than treating each screen separately. This is the same approach the built-in Windows Magnifier's full-screen mode uses.

How do I zoom on a laptop with no mouse wheel?

Use the keyboard. Hold Ctrl + Alt and press Plus to zoom in or Minus to zoom out; hold the key to keep zooming. Ctrl + Alt + 0 resets to normal. No mouse wheel or external mouse is needed, so the app works on a laptop with only its trackpad.

Does it work in games and full-screen video?

It works almost everywhere. Low Vision Zoom magnifies the entire Windows desktop, so it applies to your browser, email, documents, video playing in a window, and the Windows shell itself. The exception is a game or app that takes exclusive control of the full screen and bypasses the normal desktop; that content may not be magnified. Running such a game in windowed or borderless-window mode restores magnification.

How much can Low Vision Zoom magnify the screen?

Up to 5×, in half-step increments. This is a deliberate limit for a small, comfortable zoom tool, and it is lower than the built-in Windows Magnifier (up to 16×) or the full suites like ZoomText (up to 36×). If you need more than 5×, Low Vision Zoom is not the right tool for you — the comparison page points you to ones that go higher.
